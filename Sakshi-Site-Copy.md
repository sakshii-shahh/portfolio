# 07 · Case · IBM Deal Registration

URL: case-ibm-deal-registration.html  |  Hero image, project banner, 6 sections via TOC, interactive mockup, impact stats, light testimonial, next link.

**Hero tags**: UX RESEARCH · SHIPPED

**Title**
Redesigning Deal Registration at IBM

**Lede**
I led the redesign of IBM's **partner deal registration** flow on the Partner Plus platform — moving sellers from intent to closed deal **30% faster** across 200+ enterprise accounts. I owned end-to-end research, design, and rollout, partnering closely with the Partner Cloud engineering team.

**Visit button**: Visit IBM Partner Plus  →

**Project banner (4 columns)**
- Role: UX Researcher / Product Designer
- Team: with IBM Partner Cloud
- Timeline: Jan – Aug 2025  (8 months)
- Skills: UX Research / Service Design / Product Design

## 01 · Context
**Section title**: Partners were the biggest revenue lever — and the biggest churn risk.

IBM's partner ecosystem drives a meaningful share of enterprise revenue. But the existing deal registration tool — the system partners use to claim a sales opportunity — had grown organically over a decade. By 2024, partners were quietly going around it: emailing reps, working in spreadsheets, or simply not registering deals at all.

Internally, this looked like **missing pipeline visibility**. From the partner side, it looked like a bureaucratic gate that didn't pay them back. Both sides were losing.

## 02 · Research
**Section title**: Where the friction actually lived

I ran a six-week discovery sprint with 18 partner reps across SI, ISV, and reseller archetypes — pairing interview transcripts with backend telemetry from the existing tool. Three patterns showed up over and over:

1. Partners abandoned mid-flow when fields they didn't have data for were marked required.
2. Approval status was opaque — reps would re-submit the same deal three times to "wake it up."
3. The form treated every deal like a net-new enterprise opportunity, even for $5k SaaS renewals.

The synthesis became a tight problem statement: **the form didn't match the shape of the work.**

## 03 · The solution
**Section title**: A flow that adapts to the deal — not the other way around.

The redesign reorganized the flow around **three deal archetypes** partners actually run: renewal, expansion, and net-new. Each archetype unlocked a different field set, with smart defaults pulled from CRM. Status moved from a single hidden flag to a visible timeline, so reps always knew what was happening and what they could do about it.

We also stripped the registration form down to its essentials. **Required fields dropped from 24 to 9** on the most common path. Everything else became conditional, postponed, or pre-filled.

## 04 · Key flows
**Section title**: Designed around the moments partners actually had time for.

Three flows did the heaviest lifting. Each was tested with reps in low-fi mocks before any pixel went into the design system.

### Feature cards (interactive — hover swaps the phone mockup)
- **01 Quick-register a renewal** — Pre-fills 80% of fields from the existing customer record. A rep can register and submit in under 60 seconds.
- **02 Status timeline** — Replaces the legacy approved/pending/rejected dropdown with a live timeline showing exactly what's happened and what's next.
- **03 Save, share, hand off** — Reps can save mid-form drafts and share them with a co-rep — fixing the #1 complaint about the legacy flow.
- **04 Conditional fields** — The form asks for what's relevant, when it's relevant. Fields appear only after the deal type clarifies what's needed.

## 05 · Impact
**Section title**: What changed when partners stopped routing around us.

The redesign rolled out to a 12-account pilot, then expanded to 200+ accounts over Q3. Three months post-launch, the numbers told the story.

### Impact cards
- **30%  faster cycle time** — From first registration to closed-won, measured across 200+ accounts.
- **62%  less form abandonment** — Required-field reduction (24 → 9) was the biggest single lever.
- **200+ enterprise accounts** — Rolled out from a 12-account pilot to the full Partner Plus footprint.

> "The timeline alone saved me three follow-up emails this week. I can finally see what's actually happening with my deal."
> — D. Patel · Partner Rep, mid-market SI

## 06 · Reflection
**Section title**: What I'd do differently next time

The biggest unlock wasn't the form redesign — it was the **archetype framing**. Once we stopped treating "a deal" as one shape and started designing around the three real shapes, every other decision got easier. I'd push to find that framing earlier next time, even at the cost of slower discovery.

The thing I'd revisit: we deferred the admin-side dashboard to a later phase. With more time I'd have designed both sides in parallel — the partner experience can only be as fast as the IBM-side review, and that loop is still the rate-limiter.

# 08 · Case · Money20

URL: case-money20.html  |  Same template as IBM — refine all fields below.

- **Tags**: GAME DESIGN · SHIPPED
- **Title**: Designing Money20 — financial literacy for teens
- **Lede**: I co-designed Money20, a financial-literacy game for teens with HumanX — turning abstract money concepts into a playable narrative played by 12,000+ students in pilot. I led game design, narrative structure, and player research.
- **Visit button**: Try the Money20 demo
- **Banner** — Role: Game Designer / UX Researcher  |  Team: with HumanX learning team  |  Timeline: Jun – Dec 2024 (7 months)  |  Skills: Game Design / Narrative / Research

### 01 Context — Teens think money is boring — until it's a story.
Financial literacy classes have a retention problem. Most content reads like a textbook; most apps gamify with points instead of meaning. We wanted a third option: a narrative-driven game where money decisions actually move the plot.

### 02 Research — Where teens actually opt in
I ran playtests with 32 students aged 13–17 across three school districts. The pattern was clear — they wanted agency, characters they could see themselves in, and stakes that felt real (not just "score points").

### 03 The solution — A choose-your-own-adventure where every choice has a price.
Money20 follows three teen characters making real money decisions — first job, first emergency, first big purchase. Players are never told what's right; the consequences play out across the story.

### 04 Key flows — Three loops, each tested with kids before code.
Three core flows did the heaviest lifting. Each was paper-prototyped with players before any pixel went into Unity.

- **Banner** — Role: Brand · Web Designer  |  Team: with DP House founders  |  Timeline: 2024 (solo · 8 weeks)  |  Skills: Brand / Web Design / Front-end

### 01 Context — A studio that overdelivered in person, underdelivered online.
DP House had a strong word-of-mouth reputation but a portfolio site that read as a generic agency template. Inquiries were dropping. The disconnect was costing them work.

### 02 Research — Listening to their actual voice
Three founder conversations. Two pages of language they actually used to describe the work. One clear realization: the studio was funny, sharp, and specific — and none of that was on the site.

### 03 Solution — A site that reads like the room they're in.
Editorial layout, the founders' actual voice in the copy, project pages that lead with the why instead of the deliverable. Bold typography, generous space, and zero stock-photo energy.

# 11 · Case · Khaana Daan

URL: case-khaana-daan.html

- **Tags**: SOCIAL IMPACT · CONCEPT
- **Title**: Khaana Daan — surplus food, redirected
- **Lede**: Khaana Daan is a concept that connects surplus restaurant food with people who need it most — before it gets thrown out. Solo conceptual project exploring service design for impact.
- **Visit button**: Read the concept brief
- **Banner** — Role: Designer / Researcher  |  Team: solo concept  |  Timeline: 2023  |  Skills: Service Design / Research / Storytelling

### 01 Context — A third of restaurant food is thrown out. A third of urban Indians are food-insecure.
Two facts that don't belong in the same sentence. Existing apps either focus on consumer food delivery or on big-batch surplus from corporates — leaving the everyday restaurant gap unaddressed.

### 02 Research — Both sides of the counter
I interviewed 8 small restaurant owners and 6 NGO field workers in Bombay. The blockers weren't logistical — they were emotional and operational: dignity, predictability, and the awkwardness of asking.

### 03 Solution — A pickup-by-default service for restaurants who want to help.
Restaurants set a daily surplus window. Verified pickup partners (NGO-backed) collect within 90 minutes. Recipients get a notification, not a queue. The whole thing runs on opt-in defaults, not asks.

### 04 Key flows — Three flows, three audiences.
Restaurant, partner, recipient — each gets exactly the surface they need.
