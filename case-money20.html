Game Design Shipped

Money20

"Live 20 years in 20 minutes."

The Money20 tagline

Money20 is an investment simulation game built at HumanX for a major FinTech education client. It doesn't just teach young adults about investing, it forces them to confront their psychological biases in a safe environment. The final product simulates 20 years of investing in a 20-minute session, currently live at humanx.io. As UX Design Intern, I owned research, information architecture, and the design decisions that made the behavioral framing land.

Context

Traditional financial education is failing.

It's too theoretical. It teaches the names of concepts without the feel of them. Our target demographic, young adults in India, was paralyzed by two forces.

🧩
Complexity of products Mutual funds, ETFs, equities, bonds. The first decision was usually freezing.
😬
Fear of irreversible loss The biggest learning moments happen with real money. Users never got a safe rehearsal.

The brief wasn't to teach what to buy. It was to tackle how people behave when money is on the line, before they had real money on the line.

Research

Where the friction actually lived.

The problem

Traditional investment education lacks engagement and fails to give users a realistic sense of consequences. For young adults grasping risk tolerance and long-term implications at the same time, the abstraction is the blocker.

The goal

Build a tool that integrates behavioral finance concepts, loss aversion and herd mentality especially, directly into gameplay. A unique value proposition in a market full of static educational tools.

The business angle

Higher engagement and retention than competing platforms positioned the client as an innovator in financial education, not just another LMS.

Solution

A game that turns behavior into curriculum.

Money20 integrates behavioral finance directly into the play loop. Players make 20 years of investing decisions in 20 minutes. The system surfaces their behavioral patterns back to them as they play, so the learning happens through experience rather than after it.

The design system

The interface depended on the game's narrative. With an inevitable amount of financial information on every screen, the UI had to require very little cognitive effort.

Features

Five features doing most of the work.

Competitive and single-player modes

Players can compete head-to-head or go solo against the computer.

  • Why. Solo mode removes performance anxiety while players learn. Competitive mode introduces the herd-mentality variable we wanted them to feel.
  • Outcome. Same content layer, two ways in.

Scaffolded financial products

Investments unlock over time. Each unlock pairs with a behavioral nudge so players grasp their own risk tolerance, not abstract definitions.

  • Why. Research showed users froze when shown all products at once.
  • Outcome. Sequenced unlocks tied to in-game progression let players build literacy without overwhelm.

The Elephant Hint

If a player hasn't made a move for more than two minutes, an in-game elephant prompts them with a helper tip.

  • Why. Stuck players disengage. The nudge catches the "I don't know what to do" moment before churn.
  • Outcome. After iteration on copy and timing, a 20% lift in engagement with the guidance system.

Follow the best investors

Players see how top performers in their cohort structured their portfolios so they can apply the same strategies.

  • Why. Social proof is a real-world driver. Modeling it inside the game lets players feel the bias being formed.
  • Outcome. Players who studied the leaderboards iterated their strategies faster.

Real-life situations

Emergency expenses (medical bills, unemployment) and positive breakers (festive bonus, gifts) keep stakes real.

  • Why. Excluding real-world shocks sanitizes the simulation. The lesson lives in the liquidity scramble.
  • Outcome. Players locked into long-term investments learned why an emergency fund matters.

Impact

Validation, iteration, and a shipped product.

My responsibility didn't end with design. It extended to validating the design worked and influencing what shipped next.

Usability testing and refinement

I ran unmoderated usability testing on a clickable prototype. The most significant finding was user confusion around the generic "Elephant Hint", users didn't understand the motivation behind the prompt. We iterated copy and timing, and the new version made the nudge feel like guidance rather than interruption.

Reflection

Connecting design to behavioral strategy.

Money20 was my first time designing a system where the goal was behavior change, not task completion. The framework I picked up here has shown up in every project since.

01

The smallest interaction holds the most weight

The Elephant Hint felt like a small UX problem until testing showed it was the single biggest churn risk. In a behavioral product, the smallest interactions carry the most weight.

Feature priority
02

Iteration over instinct

The 20% engagement lift wasn't an insight. It was a copy and timing tweak that came out of testing. The system was right. The execution needed iteration.

Process
03

The lesson lives in the loss

Players learned more from a 30-second bad decision than any tutorial could deliver. Designing for productive failure became a craft I've been refining ever since.

Craft

The best behavior-change products don't teach. They let you teach yourself.
