Social Impact Concept

Khaana Daan, surplus food, redirected

Khaana Daan is a concept that connects surplus restaurant food with people who need it most, before it gets thrown out. Solo conceptual project exploring service design for impact.

Context

A third of restaurant food is thrown out. A third of urban Indians are food-insecure.

Two facts that don't belong in the same sentence. Existing apps either focus on consumer food delivery or on big-batch surplus from corporates, leaving the everyday restaurant gap unaddressed.

Internally, this looked like missing pipeline visibility. From the partner side, it looked like a bureaucratic gate that didn't pay them back. Both sides were losing.

Research

Both sides of the counter

I interviewed 8 small restaurant owners and 6 NGO field workers in Bombay. The blockers weren't logistical, they were emotional and operational: dignity, predictability, and the awkwardness of asking.

  1. Partners abandoned mid-flow when fields they didn't have data for were marked required.
  2. Approval status was opaque, reps would re-submit the same deal three times to "wake it up."
  3. The form treated every deal like a net-new enterprise opportunity, even for $5k SaaS renewals.

The synthesis became a tight problem statement: the form didn't match the shape of the work.

The solution

A pickup-by-default service for restaurants who want to help.

Restaurants set a daily surplus window. Verified pickup partners (NGO-backed) collect within 90 minutes. Recipients get a notification, not a queue. The whole thing runs on opt-in defaults, not asks.

Before, flat form
After, archetype-led flow

We also stripped the registration form down to its essentials. Required fields dropped from 24 to 9 on the most common path. Everything else became conditional, postponed, or pre-filled.

Key flows

Three flows, three audiences.

Restaurant, partner, recipient, each gets exactly the surface they need.

01 Quick-register a renewal

Pre-fills 80% of fields from the existing customer record. A rep can register and submit in under 60 seconds.

02 Status timeline

Replaces the legacy approved/pending/rejected dropdown with a live timeline showing exactly what's happened and what's next.

03 Save, share, hand off

Reps can save mid-form drafts and share them with a co-rep, fixing the #1 complaint about the legacy flow.

04 Conditional fields

The form asks for what's relevant, when it's relevant. Fields appear only after the deal type clarifies what's needed.

Reflection

What I'd do differently next time

The biggest unlock wasn't the form redesign, it was the archetype framing. Once we stopped treating "a deal" as one shape and started designing around the three real shapes, every other decision got easier. I'd push to find that framing earlier next time, even at the cost of slower discovery.

The thing I'd revisit: we deferred the admin-side dashboard to a later phase. With more time I'd have designed both sides in parallel, the partner experience can only be as fast as the IBM-side review, and that loop is still the rate-limiter.
